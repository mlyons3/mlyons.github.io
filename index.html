Mice Protein Expression Analysis¶

Phil Rinaldi and Molly Lyons, CMPS 3160

Introduction

For our project, we will investigate a dataset on mice protein expression that we found on Kaggle (https://www.kaggle.com/ruslankl/mice-protein-expression). This dataset contains information on proteins that are expressed in several conditions in mice, including when mice are treated with the drug memantine (a drug used to treat Alzheimer's disease) if they are stimulated to learn or not stimulated to learn. We want to use the data to determine the effect memantine and different types of learning have on the mice. The data contains 1080 measurements of expression per protein, for 72 mice (and each mouse had 15 measurements taken on it). The data has already been neatly categorized by the experimental conditions to which the mice were subjected and their biological traits (specifically trisomic or control gene, injected/not injected with memantine, stimulated/not stimulated to learn). We will only be looking at the control mice.

The dataset was in tidy form when we downloaded it, so not much ETL needed to be performed initially. All columns are named properly and are not variables, all empty values are set to NaN, each variable forms a row, all variables have their own column, and there is only one type of observational unit in the table. Otherwise, we had no challenges formatting the data since Kaggle had already done a very nice job making sure it was tidy. Downloading the data was also a very easy process and required no web scraping or tricky extraction.

Looking at the dataframe, there are 15 measurements for each mouse. For our future analysis we will be taking the mean of the 15 measurements for each mouse in each class as well as the average protein expression in a class of mice. There are 77 proteins expressed and 72 mice total.

Exploratory Analysis

As stated above, we will begin by taking the mean of the 15 measurements of protein expression for each mouse. First, however, a bit more manipulation of the data is necessary. The MouseID column contains two bits of information -- the mouse that was experimented on, and the number of the measurement that any particular row represents (i.e. 309_1 denotes the first measurement taken on mouse 309). We split this column into two columns -- one for the mouse and one for the measurement number -- so that we could then easily group by mouse and take the average of the protein expression data over the 15 trials.

Explaining the Data

The data that we will be using in this project only pertains to the control mice, which are normal mice with no underlying health issues. We will not be using the Ts65Dn (down syndrome) mice in our analysis. There are several other variables including treatment and learning. A mouse can either be treated by memantine, which is a drug used to treat alzheimer's disease, or saline, which has no effect and is used as the control. Mice can also be stimulated to learn from context-shock associative learning, or not stimulated to learn from shock-context associative learning. So we will be working with 4 classes of mice:

  • Control-Context Shock-Saline (c_CS_s)
  • Control-Context Shock-Memantine (c_CS_m)
  • Control-Shock Context-Saline (c_SC_s)
  • Control-Shock Context-Memantine (c_SC_m)

In each of these 4 classes of mice, there are 77 protein whose expression level in the nuclear fraction of the cortex, is measured. The amoutn of expression is already standardized across all classes and mice.

We will begin by examining the differences in protein expression for the control mice who were stimulated to learn when given either saline or memantine. The two classes of interest are thus c-CS-s and c-CS-m.

Further Analysis on Some of the Proteins

We have picked a few proteins to further analyze to demonstrate what we can learn from our findings and how they can stimulate lots of future research.

NR2A¶
  • NR2A expression is increased in mice that are stimulated to learn (fear conditioning) compared to those who are not stimulated to learn. NR2A is a protein called glutamate receptor, ionotropic, N-methyl D-aspartate(NMDA) 2A. This protein is one of two subunits in an ionotropic NMDA receptor. It has been proven that NMDA receptors are needed for proper fear memory, so an increase in NMDA receptors would indicate proper fear memory storage. So if NR2A is increased in mice that are stimulated to learn fear, it supports evidence that an increase in NMDA receptors demonstrates proper fear memory storage, and that mice stimulated to learn fear are possibly increasing their amount of NMDA receptors to store more fear memory.
    • https://www.uniprot.org/uniprot/P35436
    • https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3703758/
pCAMKII¶
  • pCAMKII is more highly expressed in mice not stimulated to learn, especially in mice treated with memantine. pCAMKII is a protein named Calcium/calmodulin-dependent protein kinase type II. This protein has shown to be an important factor in learning and memory. An excerpt from an article in Nature states, "Calcium- and calmodulin-dependent protein kinase II (CaMKII) and glutamate receptors are integrally involved in forms of synaptic plasticity that may underlie learning and memory" (Bayer, KU., 2001). CAMKII is activated by Ca2+, which enters the cell through NMDA receptors. Memantine is NMDA receptor antagonist, meaning it prevents glutamate from binding to the receptor, which reduces glutamate transmission. We would hypothesize that with decreased glutamate transmission, there would be less Cat2+ to activate CAMKII, but something about a mouse being not stimulated to learn contradicts this hypothesis. Since both CAMKII and NMDA receptors are involved in memory and learning, not being stimulated to learn clearly affects CAMKII expression levels. One way this might be possible is that the mice that are not stimulated to learn likely have a decreased amount of NMDA receptors than those who are stimulated to learn. So when memantine is blocking glutamate transmission in the brain, it is at such a low level that the brain will make more NMDA receptors. So it's possible that the increased NMDA receptors greatly outnumbers the amount of memantine in the brain, so there are a large amount of unblocked NMDA receptors that are producing a Ca2+ influx, which activates a large amount of CAMKII. This is just one theory based on the information I have discovered from this dataset and some outside research, so this hypothesis would need to thoroughly investigated before any conclusions could be made. Learning about this protein and how it's affected by learning and memantine future research.
    • https://www.nature.com/articles/35081080
    • https://www.uniprot.org/uniprot/P28652
    • Bayer, KU., De Koninck, P., Leonard, A. et al. Interaction with the NMDA receptor locks CaMKII in an active conformation. Nature 411, 801805 (2001). https://doi.org/10.1038/35081080
CaCN¶
  • CaCN is a protein called voltage-dependent calcium channel subunit alpha-2/delta-3. It is more highly expressed in mice that were stimulated to learn. In mice that were both stimulated and not stimulated to learn, the saline treatment had a higher level of expression than the memantine expression. This leads us to believe that memantine decreases CaCN expression, whether the mouse has expirienced context-shock (simulated to learn) or shock-context (not stimulated to learn). There still was overall higher CaCN expression in mice that had been stimulated to learn, which demonstrates that shock-context somehow increases CaCN expression. It has been proven that CaCN doesn't directly interact with NMDA receptors, but when NMDA receptors are activated by glutamate, calcium channels, which CaCN is a part of, let Ca2+ flow into the cell. Through our research it does not look like anyone has researched the relationship between the CaCN subunit and learning and memory in the brain, so this could be an opportunity for research in the future.
